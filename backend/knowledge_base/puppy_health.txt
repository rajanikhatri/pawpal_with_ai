Schedule the first vet visit within a few days of bringing a puppy home.
Bring adoption records, breeder records, vaccine dates, deworming history, and current food information.
Puppies usually receive core vaccines every 2-4 weeks until about 16 weeks of age.
Core dog vaccines commonly protect against distemper, adenovirus, parvovirus, and rabies.
Many veterinarians also recommend parainfluenza and leptospirosis based on risk and local guidance.
Rabies vaccination timing depends on local law and vaccine label directions.
Parvovirus is especially dangerous for puppies and can cause severe vomiting, diarrhea, dehydration, and death.
Avoid dog parks and unknown unvaccinated dogs until the puppy vaccine series is complete.
Watch for vomiting, diarrhea, coughing, trouble breathing, weakness, pale gums, bloating, or collapse.
Call a veterinarian urgently for bloody diarrhea, repeated vomiting, seizures, or refusal to eat.
Lethargy, fever, nasal discharge, eye discharge, or sudden behavior changes can signal illness.
Young puppies can become dehydrated quickly, so do not wait long if symptoms are worsening.
Deworming is commonly started early because intestinal parasites are common in puppies.
Veterinarians may check a stool sample for roundworms, hookworms, whipworms, coccidia, or giardia.
Flea, tick, and heartworm prevention should be chosen with a veterinarian based on age and weight.
Never use adult dog parasite products on puppies unless the label and veterinarian say it is safe.
Discuss spay or neuter timing with a veterinarian because breed, size, health, and behavior all matter.
Many small dogs are altered around 6 months, while some large breeds may benefit from waiting longer.
Handle ears, paws, mouth, and collar gently every day to prepare for care and vet exams.
Brush puppy teeth daily if possible using dog-safe toothpaste only.
Human toothpaste can be unsafe for dogs and should not be used.
Puppy baby teeth are usually replaced by adult teeth by about 6 months.
Provide safe chew toys to reduce chewing on furniture and help with teething discomfort.
Keep medications, cleaning products, toxic foods, and small swallowable objects out of reach.
Call a veterinarian or pet poison hotline immediately if toxin exposure is suspected.
Regular wellness visits help track growth, body condition, vaccine needs, and behavior concerns.
