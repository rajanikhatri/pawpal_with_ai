Start potty training as soon as the puppy comes home.
Take the puppy outside first thing in the morning and last thing at night.
Also go outside after meals, after drinking water, after naps, and after play.
At 8-12 weeks, many puppies need a potty break about every 1-2 hours while awake.
At 3-4 months, many puppies can wait about 2-3 hours during the day.
At 5-6 months, many puppies can wait about 3-4 hours, but accidents may still happen.
Use the same outdoor spot so smell cues help the puppy understand the routine.
Reward immediately after the puppy finishes outside, not after returning indoors.
Use praise, small treats, and calm excitement for positive reinforcement.
Never punish accidents after the fact because the puppy will not connect the punishment to the behavior.
Clean accidents with an enzymatic cleaner to remove odor cues.
Teach "sit" by moving a treat slowly upward and rewarding when the hips lower.
Teach "stay" by rewarding one second of stillness, then slowly increasing time and distance.
Teach "come" with a happy voice, short distance, and high-value rewards.
Teach "down" by luring the nose toward the floor and rewarding relaxed elbows.
Keep sessions short: 3-5 minutes for young puppies, several times per day.
Crate training works best when the crate is safe, quiet, and never used as punishment.
The crate should be large enough to stand, turn around, and lie down comfortably.
Introduce the crate with meals, treats, soft bedding, and short calm sessions.
The key socialization window is roughly 8-16 weeks of age.
Socialization means safe exposure to people, sounds, surfaces, handling, and friendly vaccinated animals.
Avoid unknown dogs and high-risk public areas until vaccines are complete.
Common mistakes include waiting too long to train, using punishment, expecting adult bladder control, and skipping rewards.
Consistency from every family member helps puppies learn faster.
If fear, biting, or accidents are severe, ask a veterinarian or certified positive-reinforcement trainer for help.
