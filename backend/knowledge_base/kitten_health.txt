Schedule the first vet visit within a few days of bringing a kitten home.
Bring adoption records, vaccine dates, deworming history, and current food information.
Kittens commonly visit the veterinarian every 3-4 weeks until about 16-20 weeks of age.
Core kitten vaccines include feline viral rhinotracheitis, calicivirus, and panleukopenia, often called FVRCP.
Rabies vaccination is core in many areas and timing depends on local law and vaccine label directions.
Feline leukemia virus vaccination is recommended as core for cats younger than 1 year by AAHA/AAFP guidelines.
Veterinarians may recommend FeLV testing before vaccination or when history is unknown.
Keep kittens away from unknown cats until vaccines and parasite checks are current.
Watch for poor appetite, vomiting, diarrhea, sneezing, eye discharge, coughing, or trouble breathing.
Other warning signs include lethargy, pale gums, limping, bloated belly, dehydration, or hiding more than usual.
Kittens can decline quickly, so call a veterinarian promptly if symptoms persist or worsen.
Deworming is common because roundworms, hookworms, and other parasites can affect kittens.
A stool sample can help check for intestinal parasites such as coccidia or giardia.
Use flea, tick, and parasite prevention only if it is labeled for the kitten's age and weight.
Never use dog flea products on cats because some ingredients are highly toxic to cats.
Discuss spay or neuter timing with a veterinarian; many kittens are altered around 4-6 months.
Earlier surgery may be appropriate in shelters or rescue settings based on veterinary protocols.
Handle paws, ears, mouth, and tail gently to prepare the kitten for grooming and exams.
Brush regularly for long-haired kittens to prevent mats.
Start nail trims with tiny tips only, using calm rewards and short sessions.
Dental care can begin with gentle mouth handling and cat-safe toothpaste when appropriate.
Do not use human toothpaste for cats.
Keep lilies away from cats because even small exposures can be life-threatening.
Store medications, cleaning products, strings, rubber bands, and small objects safely.
Call a veterinarian or pet poison hotline immediately if poisoning or string swallowing is suspected.
