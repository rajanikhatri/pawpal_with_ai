Set up a kitten in one quiet starter room for the first few days.
Include food, water, bedding, toys, hiding places, scratching surfaces, and a litter box.
Place the litter box away from food and water but close enough to find easily.
Use a low-sided box for small kittens so they can climb in without difficulty.
Unscented, soft litter is often easiest for kittens to accept.
Put the kitten in the litter box after meals, after naps, and after play.
Praise calm litter box use and keep the box clean every day.
Do not punish litter accidents because stress can make litter problems worse.
If accidents continue, check box location, cleanliness, litter type, and possible illness.
Introduce the rest of the home gradually after the kitten is eating, playing, and using the box reliably.
Provide vertical spaces such as cat trees, shelves, or safe window perches.
Scratch post training works best when posts are sturdy and placed near sleeping and play areas.
Offer both vertical and horizontal scratchers because cats have different preferences.
Reward scratching posts with treats, praise, play, or catnip if age appropriate.
Redirect furniture scratching by calmly moving the kitten to an approved scratcher.
Daily play helps kittens practice hunting skills and reduces biting and pouncing on people.
Use wand toys, balls, tunnels, and puzzle feeders instead of hands as toys.
If a kitten bites or scratches, pause play and redirect to a toy.
Avoid rough hand wrestling because it teaches kittens that skin is a toy.
Short socialization sessions help kittens accept handling, carriers, brushing, nail trims, and visitors.
Cats can learn cues such as come, sit, target, high five, and carrier using rewards.
Clicker training can work well because it marks the exact behavior being rewarded.
Keep sessions short, often 1-3 minutes, and stop before the kitten loses interest.
Never force interaction; let the kitten approach and retreat safely.
Sudden aggression, hiding, or litter changes may signal stress or illness and should be evaluated.
