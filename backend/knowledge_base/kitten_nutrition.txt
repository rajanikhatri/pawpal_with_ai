Kittens need food labeled complete and balanced for growth or all life stages.
After weaning, kittens usually need several small meals because their stomachs are small.
At 6-12 weeks, many kittens do best with 4 meals per day.
At 3-6 months, many kittens can eat 3 meals per day.
At 6-12 months, many kittens can eat 2 meals per day, though some still prefer smaller meals.
Kittens should eat kitten-specific food rather than adult maintenance food.
Cats are obligate carnivores and need high-quality animal protein.
AAFCO growth diets for cats contain at least 30% protein on a dry matter basis.
Kittens also need taurine, arachidonic acid, vitamin A, and other nutrients found in balanced cat food.
Wet food can support hydration and may be easier for some kittens to chew.
Dry food can be convenient, but it should not be the only water source.
Many families feed a mix of wet and dry kitten food if the kitten tolerates both.
Use the package feeding guide and adjust for weight gain, body condition, and veterinary advice.
Kittens should grow steadily without becoming round or pot-bellied from overfeeding.
Fresh water should be available at all times in a clean bowl.
Some kittens drink more from a fountain or a wide shallow bowl away from the litter box.
Cow's milk is not recommended because many kittens and cats are lactose intolerant.
Onions, garlic, chives, chocolate, alcohol, caffeine, and xylitol are unsafe for cats.
Raw fish can carry bacteria and may contain enzymes that interfere with thiamine if fed often.
Raw meat, raw eggs, and bones can expose kittens to bacteria, parasites, or injuries.
Dog food is not appropriate for kittens because it lacks key feline nutrients.
Treats should be small and should not replace balanced kitten meals.
Most cats transition from kitten food to adult cat food between 8 and 12 months.
Large or slow-maturing cats may need individualized timing from a veterinarian.
Transition foods gradually over 5-7 days to reduce vomiting or diarrhea.
Ask a veterinarian before feeding homemade, vegan, raw, or supplement-heavy diets.
